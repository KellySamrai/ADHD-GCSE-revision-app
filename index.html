Why this works

The actual neuroscience — not a lecture.

⏱ Time blindness

Your brain sorts time into two buckets: NOW and NOT NOW. "Revise at 5pm" doesn't feel real until it's already gone. This is neurological — the ADHD brain has reduced dopamine in the prefrontal cortex, which is the part that makes future time feel tangible.

→ Every session here is 20 minutes with a visible countdown. The timer makes time visible and concrete — your brain can work with that. It's short enough that starting doesn't feel impossible.

🔥 Interest-driven brain

The ADHD brain runs on a different motivation system. Neurotypical brains can push through boring tasks using willpower. The ADHD brain needs interest, urgency, challenge, or novelty to engage the dopamine system — without those, it literally cannot sustain focus. That's not laziness. That's how your brain is wired.

→ This plan uses videos, questions, drawing, and colour instead of textbooks. Subjects you care about are scheduled first to build momentum into harder ones. Each session is different to create novelty.

🧪 What actually works for revision

Most people revise wrong. Re-reading notes and highlighting feel productive but research shows only around 10% actually sticks. The ADHD brain especially struggles to encode passive reading because there's no dopamine reward from it.

→ Every topic in this app has practice questions. Testing yourself is 4–5× more effective than re-reading (retrieval practice). Spacing revision over weeks beats cramming. Explaining things out loud beats reading in silence.

📐 Why 20 minutes?

Research on ADHD attention shows that sustained focus typically peaks around 15–20 minutes before degrading. A Pomodoro-style structure with a defined endpoint also reduces the cognitive cost of starting — "just 20 minutes" is less threatening than "revise for an hour".

→ After each session, take at least 5 minutes away from the screen. Walk, get water, do nothing. This is when the brain consolidates what it just learned.

The target
A grade 4 is a standard pass. A grade 5 is the maximum on Foundation papers (Maths and Science). This plan is built around reaching those targets — not around being perfect, and not around revising in ways that don't work.

What is Irlen Syndrome?

Some people's brains process light differently. White pages, bright screens, and certain lighting can make text look like it's moving, blurring, or swimming. It's not your eyes — it's how your brain handles what your eyes send it. A coloured overlay filters out the wavelengths that cause the problem. It won't fix everything, but it can make reading less tiring.

Tips for reading off-screen
  • Use a coloured overlay sheet on paper — purple or blue usually works best
  • Try a reading ruler to isolate one line at a time
  • Avoid pure white paper. Cream or pastel-coloured paper is easier
  • Increase font size and line spacing on any digital text you can control
  • Avoid fluorescent lighting when possible — daylight or warm lamps are better
  • Take a break every 15–20 minutes when reading on paper
